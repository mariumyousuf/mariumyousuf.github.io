Research

My advisors are Jean-Marc Fellous and Michael Chertkov. We primarily investigate replay events in the brain, which are the reactivation of neural sequences during rest and sleep resembling the sequences from prior exploratory behavior. The main research questions we are tackling are:

  • How replay can be detected from neuron population activity using principled causal methods?
  • How do replay mechanisms offer insights into continual learning in artificial intelligence?

PhD Dissertation (Summer 2026)

    Title: Inference of Effective Connectivity Graphs from Neural Replay Spike Trains

    Hippocampal replay reflects the reactivation of neural activity patterns during sleep and rest that resemble prior wakeful experience. In spatial navigation tasks, this replay is thought to support memory consolidation and long-term retrieval. However, how replay relates to the underlying functional and effective connectivity of neural circuits remains unclear. Understanding this relationship can provide insight into how memories are encoded, organized, and propagated through neural networks.

    We simulate activity in a biologically realistic CA3 place cell network using the NEURON simulation environment, implementing single-compartment pyramidal neurons with AMPA and NMDA synapses and in vivo-like stochastic background excitation and inhibition. Controlled causal structure is introduced via a connectivity matrix that modulates synaptic strengths to embed known directed influences.

    To infer effective connectivity, we develop a sparse generative transition model with a two-phase prune-and-refit procedure to estimate directed interactions governing network dynamics. We benchmark this method against naive baselines (spike counting, cross-correlation), pairwise methods (Granger causality, transfer entropy), and multivariate approaches (point-process GLM, VARLiNGAM), evaluating performance against the ground-truth connectivity matrix. Robustness is assessed across variations in sparsity, synaptic strength, network size, and sample size.

Other Projects

    Title: Robust Automation for Connectomics

    Understanding brain function at the cellular level requires accurate 3D reconstruction of neuronal tissue from high-resolution microscopy data. Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM) provides nanometer-scale resolution, enabling detailed visualization of subcellular brain structures. However, constructing accurate 3D volumes from multi-tile, multi-slice datasets remains challenging due to alignment errors and the need for extensive manual correction in current toolchains.

    We analyze multi-tile TEM image datasets of mouse brain slices using reconstruction pipelines based on TrakEM2, as well as web-based visualization tools such as Neuroglancer and WebKnossos. To evaluate alignment quality, we study overlapping tile regions using both manual landmark selection and automated feature matching via Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT). We quantify alignment error using geometric fitting (elliptical contour models) and statistical analysis of feature displacements. We also test whether observed misalignments can be explained by optical distortion using a Brown–Conrady lens distortion model.

    We develop automated methods for evaluating and improving alignment in large-scale TEM datasets, reducing reliance on manual correction in 3D reconstruction workflows. Our analysis identifies systematic patterns in tile misalignment and shows that feature-based matching captures dense correspondences, while optical distortion models partially explain geometric error structure.

    Title: Bayesian inference for sparse graphical models of brain connectivity

    We study how Alzheimer’s disease affects functional connectivity in the brain using sparse Bayesian graphical models. The goal is to infer interpretable connectivity structures from high-dimensional neural data.

    We model brain connectivity using Gaussian precision matrices and develop Bayesian inference methods for sparse graph learning, including variational inference, Gibbs sampling, and Laplace approximations. We also explore structured priors such as G-Wishart distributions and scalable optimization techniques for high-dimensional settings.

    Our work demonstrates that sparse Bayesian models can recover meaningful connectivity patterns under noise and limited data, providing a framework for studying brain network degradation.

    My contributions include developing a computational framework for Bayesian inference in sparse graphical models, implementing variational and sampling-based methods, and building simulation and benchmarking pipelines for structured graph data.

    Title: Diagnosing Dementia Using Smart Chair, Wearable Device, and Testing Apps

    We study early detection of dementia by combining cognitive assessment with passive behavioral and physiological sensing. The goal is to capture subtle markers of cognitive decline that are not visible through standard screening alone.

    We developed a multimodal framework integrating a pressure-sensitive smart chair, wearable sensor data, and SLUMS cognitive scores. We analyzed data from 296 patients (including 21 with clinical ground truth), focusing on aligning behavioral signals with cognitive test responses and exploring diagnostic separability using sensor-derived features.

    • Integrated multimodal dataset combining chair, wearable, and cognitive testing data
    • Processing pipeline for heterogeneous physiological and behavioral signals
    • Temporal alignment of sensor data with SLUMS response structure
    • Exploratory analysis of pressure patterns across diagnostic groups
    • Preliminary ROC-based evaluation of diagnostic separability

    Output: Report presented at the Annual Graduate Research Symposium, Intelligent Systems Center ( Spring 2019)
